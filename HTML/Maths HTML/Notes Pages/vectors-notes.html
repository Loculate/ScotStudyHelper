Collinearity

Video Credit to MathsStatsUNSW

In mathematics, collinearity is a term used to describe certain types of points. For points to be colinear they must meet two criteria,

1. They must be parallel, which means they are multiples of the same vector, for example vectors i and 2i are parallel.

2. They must also share a common point.

Lets look at the example below, commonly a question will ask you to prove wether points are or are not colinear, for example "Since A(-2, -2), B(0, 0), C(4, 4) Prove that the vectors AB and BC are colinear"

The first step is to find vecors AB and BC, which we can do by using AB = b - a and BC = c - b. Now we have both vectors, and we can see that 2AB = BC(Since BC is two times bigger, and therefore we need two AB to equal one BC).

The last step is to state that the points are colinear, for instance,

• As 2AB = BC, the vectors are parallel.

• As they also share a common point, they are colinear."

Line Ratios

Video Credit NT Maths

Line ratios are closely related to collinearity, in that we also must find the difference in size between two vectors.

Once we have the difference in size we can then figure out some other information using algebra.

For example lets look at the question below using using the same points as last time, but this time without the point b.

"Since its known that A(-2, -2), C(4, 4) and B divides the two points in the ratio 1:2, find the coordinates of b"

"We can first begin by setting out equation to 2AB = BC, (Since we know that BC is two times bigger, we need two AB to equal it out.).

The next step is to simplify to the equation 2(b - a) = (c - b), and then 3b = c + 2a.

The last step is to sub in our points and then divide by 3 to get out point b, which is (0, 0).

Angles in Vectors

These will appear mostly on calculator papers but also occasionally on non-calculator papers, and are relatively simple. Lets look at the example below:

"Find the angle between vectors A(3, 4) and B(-2, 5)"

"Since we need both the magnitudes and dot product of the vectors, we can calculate the dot product from a.b = (3 * -2) + (4 * 5), and hence a.b = 14

To find the magnitudes, we can use │a│ = √((3)² + (4)²), and │b│ = √((-2)² + (5)²), and so │a│ = 5, and │b│ = √29.
